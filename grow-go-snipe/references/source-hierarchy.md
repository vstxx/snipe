# Source Hierarchy

Not all evidence is equal. This file ranks source reliability so verdicts rest
on the strongest available basis, and so "verified" always means "traced toward
the origin", not "found repeated somewhere". Prefer the highest tier you can
reach for any given claim; note the tier of the evidence behind each verdict.

## The tiers

### Tier 1 — Primary official and authoritative sources
The data producer or the document itself. Strongest evidence.

- National statistics offices and central banks — e.g. **GUS** (Poland),
  **Eurostat**, **NBP**, and their equivalents in other countries.
- Sector regulators and their published data — e.g. **URE** (energy, Poland),
  and comparable regulators elsewhere.
- Primary legislation and official legal texts — **EUR-Lex**, national law
  gazettes, **gov.pl** and equivalent government portals.
- Court judgments and official registries; company statutory filings and
  audited financial statements.
- Peer-reviewed primary research and the original datasets behind it.
- The primary record of a quote: official transcript, verbatim recording, the
  speaker's own published statement.

These are jurisdiction-agnostic categories; the Polish/EU bodies are named
because they are frequent primaries for Central European material, not because
the method is limited to them. Substitute the equivalent producer for whatever
jurisdiction the claim concerns (e.g. the relevant national statistics office).

### Tier 2 — Reliable secondary sources
Do original checking or aggregation with editorial standards, but sit one step
from the origin.

- Established academic literature reviews and meta-analyses.
- Reputable research institutions and international bodies (OECD, IMF, World
  Bank, IEA) reporting or compiling data.
- Major news organizations with published corrections policies and named
  reporting — used as a pointer toward the primary source, not as the endpoint.
- Think-tank publications from institutions with methodological transparency —
  but see the conflict-of-interest note below; always record their orientation.

### Tier 3 — Weak sources
Usable as leads, rarely sufficient alone for a material claim.

- Press releases and corporate/government communications (interested parties).
- Single-outlet reporting with no corroboration.
- Opinion columns, advocacy-group publications, trade-association figures.
- Wikipedia and encyclopedic aggregators — excellent for finding the primary
  source cited in the footnotes; not itself the evidence.

### Tier 4 — High-risk sources
Do not rest a verdict on these. Treat as unsourced.

- Anonymous posts, social media, forums.
- Content farms, SEO spam, AI-generated content mills.
- Sites with a documented record of fabrication or disinformation.
- Self-published material with no editorial oversight and a clear agenda.

## Working rules

- **Chase the citation down the tiers.** If a Tier-2 or Tier-3 source cites a
  Tier-1 origin, go get the Tier-1 origin and verify against it. The chain is
  only as strong as its primary link.
- **Repetition is not corroboration.** Ten outlets repeating one press release
  is one source, not ten. Independent confirmation means independently produced
  evidence, not the same claim echoed.
- **Match the source to the claim domain.** A statistic needs the statistical
  producer; a legal claim needs the statute or judgment; a scientific claim
  needs the study, not the university's press office summary of it.
- **Recency.** For any time-sensitive claim, confirm you're looking at the most
  recent release. A superseded figure from a Tier-1 source is still outdated.

## Conflict of interest and orientation

Independent of tier, flag when a source has a stake in the claim:

- The subject of a claim reporting on itself (a company's own emissions figures,
  a government's own performance statistics) — usable but interested; corroborate.
- Think tanks, advocacy groups, and industry bodies with a known ideological or
  commercial orientation. Record the orientation as context. This does **not**
  make their factual claims false — verify them on the merits — but a
  load-bearing claim sourced only to an interested party is an integrity flag
  (source risk), and a claim that conveniently serves the source's interest
  deserves extra corroboration.
- Funding relationships where disclosed and relevant.

The goal is not to dismiss sources by their politics but to calibrate how much
independent verification a claim needs before it earns a Supported verdict.
